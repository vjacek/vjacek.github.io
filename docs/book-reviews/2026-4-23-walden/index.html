Walden

April 23, 2026

Henry David Thoreau

★ ★ ★ ★ ★

Walden is an easy and obvious excellent book, and I’m glad to have finally read it for myself. There has been much and more written about the book’s greatness, and it’s all well deserved. I won’t cover much of that here.

What I will cover is something unexpected - the level of humor is wonderful:

“I have a great deal of company in my house; especially in the morning when nobody calls.” This is hilarous! His own thoughts are more interesting company than other people can provide!

Thoreau also says he let neighbors help put up the walls of his cabin, but only for their benefit - this is him making a joke, and it’s a good one.

The whole chapter called The Village is entirely about how great it is to be lost in the woods, and it’s hilarious.

Lastly, one for the Insults Hall of Fame - When talking about his “winter of lethargy” -

Never knew if it was some hereditary disease or if it was from reading Chalmers’ collection of English poetry.

Boom roasted!

I still have a few critiques though. For all his observations of the woods and it’s changing seasons, he never mentions spring peepers, a distinctly joyous and comforting springtime phenomenon that heralds warmer weather.

Thoreau also got owls wrong. Their hoot is not dismal and mournful at all. They are the powerful and calm sentinels of the woods, keeping survey from above and assuring all who can hear them, fieldmice and squirrels excepted, that the night’s darkness is a blanket of safety, stillness, and peace. For if you can hear an owl, you do not hear calamity.

The conclusion also does a lot of the philosophical heavy lifting.
