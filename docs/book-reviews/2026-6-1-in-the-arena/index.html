In The Arena

June 1, 2026

David S. Brown

★ ☆ ☆ ☆ ☆

DID NOT FINISH

“Historical events can’t really stand up by themselves and be interesting.”

– Daniel Gordon, UMass Amherst Professor of History

When I first heard this in Professor Gordon’s history class as a freshman CompSci major, it was nothing short of hilarious. It’s still hilarious, but I’ve come to appreciate that it’s also true. Historical facts are useless in isolation, and supremely boring in a long list.

But historical facts can also be analyzed, put into context, and woven through a story with a hero’s journey and villian’s motivations. Unfortunately for In the Arena, it doesn’t do any of that. The book is a simple listing of the events of TR’s life, and deftly dodges any insight into his thinking or the why behind the what. Brown is quite quality at alliteration though and he uses it often enough that I noticed in the first few pages.

Given that, it’s hard to acceptably ascertain accuracy of the book despite thinking that I’m learning quite a lot. Maybe I have, maybe I haven’t. Further research on the topic would be required, but I’m probably not that invested or interested.

A good way of judging books is if they are able to change your mind. On that front, In the Arena might earn an easy 5 stars. TR appears to be the ultimate silver-spoon walking-contradiction. But it’s abundantly apparent that Brown distinctly dislikes TR, so I’m dubious.

  • TR was kind of a poser, writing a book “Winning the West” while having no stake or part in it himself.
  • He gave up on cattle ranching as soon as the going got tough.
  • He wore skinny jeans to Albany as a junior politician and a tailored Brooks Brothers suit to war, in Florida.
  • He started at least one war because it would be a good excuse to build up the Navy.
  • He was born into a wealthy Manhattan elite family, there was no struggle or hardship in getting to the top as we have been led to believe.
  • At his position at the Civil Service Commission, really all positions, was entirely from the spoils system, and then he goes on to use it to remove others who benefitted from the spoils system. So he’s either a shrewd and cunning operator, or a hypocrite.
  • TR frequently changed his politics to make sure he was in the popular group. I’m not saying he’s wrong to do it. I get it. Look out for Number One. But he’s absolutely not the principled teetotaler we’re led to believe.
  • When waiting for boats in Tampa to take the Rough Riders to Cuba, instead of using his connections as Assistant Secretary of the Navy to rustle up some boats, he spent 3 days in a fancy hotel while his men slept in tents.
  • TR did win the Medal of Honor… But holy crap it took 4 generations of whiney babies to get him the award.
  • When running for governor of NY, TR literally got caught being a tax cheat and had someone come “fix it”.
  • He won the Nobel Peace Prize for the Portsmouth Peace Conference ending the Japanese-Russian war, but wasn’t even actually present for the negotiations; his involvement was limited to sending some telegrams and instructions.
