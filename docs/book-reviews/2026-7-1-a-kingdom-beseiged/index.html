A Kingdom Beseiged

July 1, 2026

Raymond E. Feist

★ ★ ★ ★ ★

Feist’s books always shine brightest when Pug is the nexus of the story. And yet again, Midkemia is in trouble from an unknown enemy. This book does a great job as the first of 3 in the Chaoswar Saga to establish the heros and villians while showing us more of the world than ever before.

I ususally find multiple parallel storylines to be somewhat annoying - there’s always a favorite, some in the middle ranking that I don’t mind but don’t really love, and at least one that I can’t wait to be done with whenever it shows up. I really expected the demon Child’s story to be of the last type, but it nearly became the favorite. Why? Maybe because Child learned, grew, evolved, and took interesting actions that set it along an unexpected path. I came to really want to learn more about how this storyline would intersect with the others. And although it hasn’t yet, Child’s twist at the end of the book is a great cliffhanger. And as we fly back from Texas, I can absolutely recommend finishing a cliffhanger book while flying; it’s a fantastic mental distraction to play with instead of thinking about turbulence.

One very frustrating character in the book was Martin conDoin. I suppose he should have been seen as a hero, but his extreme inaction, in the face of growing problems and then continued inaction, led me to really hate his decision making. I remember writing even back in high school, that taking some decision then action expediently was worth much more than waiting around to find the perfect solution. This mentality sometimes results in bull-in-a-china-shop actions, but I still recommend that over inaction and indecision. With the exception of fine dining enthusiests, most people like bulls anyways - as Grandpa Jacek would say, “STRONG LIKE BULL” is a good thing.

One particular scene about smuggling arms and armour felt absolutely cinematic to read - this is top-tier writing if I’ve ever seen it:

Also the corruption that was common in Kesh’s bureuocracy hid much of what was going on, since bribes were paid for falsified cargo manifests and caravan freight was signed off without inspection. Bolts of cloth turned out to have sharp, hard edges; urns were filled with herbs that had steel broad-heads attached, and pottery was made from steel with nose and cheek guards.
