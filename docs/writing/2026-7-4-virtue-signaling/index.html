Virtue Signaling

July 4, 2026

I originally wrote this on 2022-8-24, 4 years and 2 jobs ago, after my then-employer had it’s first significant round of layoffs to make Wall Street happy. I survived the layoffs, but was still furious at how it was handled. Now, as I undertake a huge new learning and transition from Staff Engineer to Engineering Manager myself, I feel like enough time has passed to separate these thoughts from the specific situation, and actually commit to holding myself to the same standard I demanded back then.

After a layoff, offers of “help” and references from retained management are shameful examples of hollow virtue signaling.

As a manager, you had a chance to help these people. You failed. You failed for 40 hours a week for 52 weeks a year. You had a chance to help these people prove their worth and productivity to the company. Not just a chance, as a manager, it was literally your job to help these people deliver value for the company.

Value is not significantly changed by a minion building Feature A or Feature B. Value is dependent on the total course the team’s efforts are set against by the manager. The failure to deliver and demonstrate this value lies obviously upon the ones who chose the path.

Don’t say that these are some of the most talented people you’ve ever worked with. That’s simply not true. Someone put together a spreadsheet, and the names you helped put on it were the lowest ranked against some metric. That metric itself is of little concern. But as a manager, you are one of the privileged few who knows the rules of the game; that metric. And you failed to develop your team to perform well against it. Yes, they are most likely nice and morally good people, but you and your peers found them lacking against the almighty unfeelling spreadsheet.

I once had the misfortune of working under a group director who bargained for a stay of layoffs with the promise to compensate by raising attrition within the next performance review cycle. This is the coward’s path. Not only have you failed to prove your team’s value, you now put the burden of worry and stress upon that same team. After layoffs, it is more clear than ever that any individual’s performance is being stack ranked against their peers, and the most reasonable option in this example of game theory is to increase your own odds of success by any means necessary. For reasonably intelligent individuals such as software engineers, this of course includes sabotaging others on the team, thereby inverting the priority of their short and long term efforts, adding more to the pending avalanche of the team’s produced value.

If cuts need to be made, it should have been you. You were the one in charge of the team direction who failed to find the correct success vector. And yet the ones following your lead are punished for your incompetence.
