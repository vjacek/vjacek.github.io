Dan Brown

If you've enjoyed some of Dan Brown's books such as _The DaVinci Code_, _Angels and Demons_, or _Digital Fortress_, this book is absolutely nothing like those fast paced thrillers with lots of puzzle solving from a heroically smart Harvard professor turned action hero. Mostly, I'm ticked off becuase the story and especially the ending twist are quite good, but it's packaged in a really garbage writing style. It's been over a decade since I've read any other Dan Brown books so I can't remember for sure, but the style of this one is so different and bad that I almost wonder if it was written by a [ghostwriter](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghostwriter_(1992_TV_series)>).

Dan Brown succeeded with his earlier work because he took you up to the edge of common knowledge and then pushed just a little bit past into the unknown where the best you could say is "yeah maybe that is real". I know the Illuminati _were_ real, maybe they still are, and maybe they have a hand in world events. Could be. Possible. But this noetics crap? It's just garbage. It's thoroughly debunked by centuries of the scientific method, and there's no actual sci-fi reason given for why this has changed. If you're the mentally deficient kind of person who thinks [ancient aliens built the pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Aliens), I've got a book to sell you.

This book is dangerously stupid. It mixes real science and history so closely with garbage noetics that it's difficult to find the actual line of reality. It does, however, do a great job illustrating the difference between Fiction, Sci Fi, and Fantasy. Problem is, plain old regular fiction is just whatever made up nonsense you feel like without the requirement to be based on either futuristic-but-plausible technology or a mystical-but-limited magic system.

Frustratingly, the book was good enough to keep me reading for 600-something pages just to find out why the Golem wanted to destroy Threshold, because on the surface, the medical technology helped him. Only in the last 100 pages does the pace really pick up - 2 of the only 3 real "puzzles" that Langdon solves come right at the end (p577 in the first-edition hardcover), with the first puzzle on page 235. I wanted to DNF this book several times, so for actually keeping me reading, it earns a very frustrated 2 stars.

Ending every single chapter with a cliffhanger is really obnoxious. And needing 100-something chapters to get through a 600-page book is just poor organization. Despite that, many chapters _still_ included multiple character viewpoints.

Sasha's big twist was very good, and using her cats as foreshadowing was actually done very well. But it's still not any kind of proof of "nonlocal consciousnes". Dan Brown had also set himself up for a slam dunk foreshadowing/reveal with Sasha's water bottle, but just never connects it.

Robert Langdon books about symbols and their hidden meanings are fun, especially when he traipses around a historical city and you can learn some fun facts about it. This book is not a Robert Langdon book about symbols and using them to figure out hidden puzzles. Symbols actually used in the book include:

- The word "prague" with a very-slightly-goofy A (Vel Spear)
- The Great Seal of the United States
- An iPhone keyboard
- Man walking up exit stairs
- Caduceus (the only one that comes with a "well actually..." explanation. Those explanations are _why_ I read your books, Mr. Dan Brown!)
- A smiley face

wait for it...

- The Starbucks logo

Much of _Secret of Secrets_ centers around saving the manuscript to an unpublished book. At the very end, Langdon suggests naming that fictional book _Secret of Secrets_. This is both annoyingly egotistical and just a lame idea. Furthermore, [the name simply stinks to begin with](https://blog.bsprague.com/books/).

Dan Brown must have gotten some huge promotional checks, every other item is branded content, especially when the brand is irrelevant.

> _Leave it to the symbologist to ruin a good cup of coffee_, Faukman thought, feeling no guilt as he took his first heavenly sip of the creamiest flat white in the city.

Oh please...

Technical Errors

- I sure hope Penguin Random House is not using a password to secure a Very Important virtual workspace in real life. PGP encryption and SSH tokens are one Google search away, just like all the other art and history you reference to build credibility in those fields.

- > Duplicated strings that hackers call copy pasta

Good try at Millenial lingo, but swing and a miss here.

- Renaming a file from "Top Secret Evidence" to "Recipes" will most definitely not hide it from a software scan. Those use something like an MD5 or SHA-1 hash of the file's _contents_ to calculate a digital fingerprint that can be easily found in a scan. Renaming files hides it from the most naive humans.

- The energy storage of Threshold is destroyed in a neat way, but nothing within Threshold is described as reasonably using a lot of energy...

- > No interference with her book...

Provided she remove a bunch of things. Dear reader, that's called _interference_.

- We all know that the [Stargate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stargate_SG-1) program exists and was rather successful, but had nothing to do with nonlocal consciousness. Well, maybe consciousness spread across the universe.

- "rooked fortress walls".... Crenelated. The word you were looking for is "crenelated". It's one of my favorites.
